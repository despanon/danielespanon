BAHARIYE BRIDGE

danielespanon

The proposal for a new bridge emerges from a critical reading of the river as infrastructure. While rivers have historically acted as connectors, in this case of Istanbul the Golden Horn operates paradoxically as a barrier, physically and perceptually separating two nearby neighborhoods. The project responds to this inversion by introducing a new crossing that not only restores pedestrian continuity, but redefines the river as a shared, active space within the urban network.
Rather than conceiving the intervention as a purely infrastructural element, the project is grounded in a detailed ecological reading of the Bahariye Islands. These islands, historically linked to leisure but now abandoned, are understood as a fragile yet valuable habitat with latent potential. Their current condition (characterized by poor soils, sparse vegetation, and minimal human disturbance) has allowed certain bird species and aquatic fauna to persist, making ecological rehabilitation a central driver of the design.

The proposal therefore operates through a dual strategy: reconnecting the city while restoring the environmental integrity of the site. The islands are transformed into a system of structured habitats, organized through concentric ecological zones that respond to species-specific requirements such as privacy, water access, and nesting conditions. This approach enables the gradual regeneration of biodiversity while carefully managing human presence. An elevated pedestrian and cycling catwalk traverses the islands, linking both shores and providing a continuous, immersive route across the river. Importantly, this infrastructure is designed to minimize impact on sensitive areas, allowing users to experience the landscape without disrupting it. In this way, the bridge becomes more than a connection—it acts as a mediating device between urban life and ecological processes. Ultimately, the project repositions the Bahariye Islands as an active interface between city and nature. By integrating mobility, landscape, and habitat restoration, it transforms a neglected and disconnected space into a resilient, multifunctional system that reinforces both urban continuity and environmental performance.
